Home-Made Moonlight

The village was not large enough to warrant many full-time guards. The army had assigned one platoon to Wenscombe, the nearest town, and from there squads were assigned to those villages that could boast a population in excess of two hundred. Todham fit this bill, and so they got five men at arms to keep watch during the night. They alternated duties: Two groups of two to patrol the darkened streets and keep curfew, and one to man the station.

Among the guards this last job was generally considered the cushiest. It meant more time indoors, sitting down. It was quiet, for the most part: apart from the tick of the clock, and the sound of the guards as they came and went, there was little that might disturb the shift. Occasionally a call might come in over the radio. If there was a down side to the duty, it was the makework. The Lieutenant, or more likely the Sergeant, was particular about keeping the guards busy. It didn’t do to give people opportunities to slack off.

Ken White, ‘Chalky’ to his mates, pulled station duty that night. He more or less had the routine off by heart, but still relied on his notebook; you could never be too careful. Not with holy terrors like Sergeant Harry Bent. Much of his duty he liked to think of as common sense: make sure the tea urn was ready for when the patrols came back, take their reports, keep the place clean and tidy, and the ledgers and files up to date. But then there were the surprises. The things in the book that didn’t make much sense.

The skywatch, for example. The guards, as Ken understood it, were kept to keep an eye on the streets. After all, the locals were there to be protected, and if there was any trouble it usually came from them too. People got a little crazy from time to time. But what sort of menace was going to come out of the sky now? No-one was flying aeroplanes any more, let alone dropping bombs from them. But the orders were specific: refer to the charts in the back, note down what constellations were visible, and compare the moon to the diagram. Estimate its phase, position, and direction, write it down in the logbook.

“Surely we already know what phase the moon will be in, Sarge,” Chalky once said. “Just look in the almanac, it’ll be there.”

Sergeant Bent gave him a look. “Just friggin’ do it, son.” He must have been deadly serious; he didn’t even shout. Lieutenant Salmon lectured the platoon on the correct use of the quadrant and the astrolabe and drilled them on which symbols to use in the logbook, made sure they knew their ascending node from their descending. Within five years, any guardsman was an amateur astronomer and could recognise all the planets visible to the naked eye.

And so, whenever he had the station, Chalky White did as he was told, ascended to the watchtower’s roof at 2230 hours and checked the night sky; and without fail he filled in the logbook. After a year or two, he began to see the point of it. Sure, there were patterns and cycles, but once in a while, if you paid attention to the log and knew what to watch for, you might notice when the moon was off by a few degrees. On one occasion, the moon’s phase skipped from waxing gibbous to waning gibbous, without going full. That spooked him. It could just have been human error, he didn’t make any of the other observations during that week, but that’s what the record said, and you’d best believe the Lieutenant called that in to Company HQ.

Ken started his maintenance check at the bottom and worked his way up. The basement. The coal hole was full, as noted by whoever took delivery that morning. Not very environmentally friendly, but since the gas shut down and electricity wasn’t reliable, who could help that? There would come a time when the coal would run out too, of course; but not today. He checked the boiler’s regulator and moved onward and upward. Ground floor. The generator still had half a tank of fuel. The radio, the station’s lifeline, still had power.

“Wenscombe HQ, this is Todham. Radio check, over.”

“Todham from Wenscombe HQ, Radio check confirm. Ready channel one, over.”

“Channel one OK, over.”

“Channel one confirm, over.”

Ken worked his way through the remaining channels, signed off, and noted the results. All very routine; no news was good news. Taking the keys, he checked the munitions lockers. Cases of rounds for the machine gun; fifty magazines of NATO standard for the rifles. Mortar rounds. All carefully counted and accounted for. Time was, they kept this all in carefully controlled environments, temperature and humidity maintained just so. Databases to allow traceability by serial number, the works. They had to scale that back now and just do their best: keep an eye on the thermometers, fill in the books. Laborious, tedious as all hell, but did anyone want to risk the stuff going missing, or worse, blowing up of its own accord? Ammo didn’t grow on trees. Especially since no-one was making any more.

First floor. When Corporal Shill told Ken about this job, he could have sworn it was a wind-up, but he did it anyway, because wind-up or not, orders were orders. When Sergeant Bent made sure to check, Ken was a little less sure he was having his chain yanked. And when the Lieutenant quizzed him as well, he realised the NCOs had voiced their concerns and chain of command decided he needed a reminder. Best not to think about it; just run through the procedure. Sweep the floor down. Compare the design painted onto the floor with the laminated piece of paper. If any parts of the design had been scuffed or flaked away, get the pot of white paint and touch it up.

He was in luck. The circles were unbroken, compass points clearly demarked, and a check with the station’s compass showed North still aligned with North. Pentagrams between each of the compass points were still clear, the word ‘TETRAGRAMMATON’ written into each star’s arms, a Tau symbol indicating where to place a candle. The serpent that wound between the circles still bore the names that had been written upon it. Chalky had to double-check with his reference picture just to make sure the names were intact, as, like the rest of the platoon, he couldn’t read Hebrew. He knew it was read from right to left, but that’s all he knew about it. Still, it was all up to spec, right down to the four hexagrams marked with ‘ADONAI’ surrounding the square at the centre of the design.

He didn’t know what was actually done with the circle; regs were very clear on the matter: Operation of the Circle of Solomon only to occur under the supervision of the commanding officer. The particulars were above his payscale, however weird they might be.

In this fashion Chalky White continued up towards the top of the tower. He yawned. That’s the funny thing about the end of the world, he thought. You’d expect it to be a lot quicker. Cataclysms. All over within a week. Not the Fall, or the Decline. The end of civilisation as you know it shouldn’t give you time to yawn, and daily lists of chores to boot.

He climbed the watchtower, yet when he turned to the window, what he saw made him tremble. Notebook in hand, he had just found his page to make a start with the night’s skywatch, turned his gaze in a moonward direction, and what he witnessed threatened to destroy his sanity.

He no longer recognised the Moon.

Much of his experience of the Fall had been passive, a series of events that never directly happened to him. All the TV channels ceased. Terrestrial television resumed broadcast, switching from digital back to analogue. Programmes on the wireless, like in the old days. And then, to save power, no more telly. The Decline. But now, here, right in front of him (give or take a quarter of a million miles), the Moon had stopped being the Moon.

It was still a white disc in the sky, in its full phase, glowing with the reflected light of the Sun. But inside that circle, things had changed. The dark patches, the maria, were no longer in a familiar shape. Chalky was no selenographer; he had heard of the Sea of Tranquillity, the Sea of Serenity, the Sea of Crises, but he couldn’t tell which was which, let alone draw them; but he’d know the pattern if he looked at it. And this pattern he did not recognise. Worse, the pattern was moving, right before his eyes, the dark patches drifting towards the centre, forming a grey disk. He stared stupidly at it for a while, trying to make sense of what he saw. If the dark areas were regions of lower elevation, perhaps that meant the surface of the moon was somehow caving in.

Ken’s stomach clenched; he felt his bowels loosen a little.

He realised he was panicking, and while he couldn’t totally stifle the urge to run away screaming, he had his training. His feet were moving before his brain caught up, and by the time he was halfway down the stairs, Chalky knew the first thing he had to do: he had to call it in.

“Wenscombe HQ from Todham. Come in, Wenscombe. Emergency, over.”

“Todham from Wenscombe. Say again, over.”

“HQ, I say again, we have an emergency. I don’t know how to describe it. It’s a… lunar event. The Moon’s gone… it’s gone wrong. Is the Lieutenant there, over?”

“White, this is Lieutenant Salmon. Describe your moon, over.

“Skywatch began at time 2230, Sir. Observed Full Moon, then noticed its seas had drifted, sir. The Sea of Tranquillity, all the seas, were covering the whole face of the Moon. Paused skywatch and called HQ. Please confirm, over.”

Seconds ticked by, each one stretched to an infinity. White was about to repeat his request, when the Lieutenant’s voice cut back in.

“HQ skywatch confirms. Convergence of dark coloration on near side of lunar surface. White, upon sign-off you are to use the Circle, over.”

“Yes, Sir. Please confirm, Sir. Regulations—”

“—regulations specify I am to supervise. I am supervising you now. You observed this first, so you have to use the Circle, over.”

“Roger that, Sir. Ready for instructions, over.”

“In the secure filing cabinet, top drawer, you will find instructions on preparing the Circle for use. You will also find a ring binder. Get those, and report back, over.”

Retrieving the keys, White dashed to the red filing cabinet, unlocked it, and yanked the top drawer open. It was the first time he’d ever looked in there; he did not expect to ever have to. He dug out the contents at the front of the drawer: a spiral-bound book, which bore a copy of the Circle on the cover; and a blue ring-binder, a label on whose spine bore the word GOETIA.

“Got them now, Sir, over.”

“Good. Open the folder. You are going to use the Circle, and what you have in the book to contact someone called (there was a pause, presumably Salmon was looking the name up) Andrealphus. That’s section sixty-five, I say again section six five, in the folder. Find that, take it out of the folder, and take section six five, the book, and a walkie-talkie up to the first floor. Take the keys, too. I will contact you shortly. Over and out.”

Private Aaliyah Gordon and Private (former Airman) Dave Hawkins had adjusted pretty well to life following the Fall. She had been in the army five years to his three, and on the whole they were glad to be there. Things had been rough. The gradual failure of technology had been especially tough; considering that Britain and England in particular had already leapt feet-first into being post-industrial. With finances in free fall, readjustment had been painful: getting any kind of manufacturing venture going was a gamble that needed government backing. People had balked; the rich hated giving up land and resources; a lot of folks in the middle had to shift from service economy and financial jobs to actually making things and learning trades; and people on the bottom, well, they were always going to have a rough time no matter what happened. With the Internet down, the smartphones bricked, and TV having guttered and sputtered to the point of there being literally nothing on, life had really started to suck. Never mind the trains running on time and costing too much to take anywhere; they hardly ran at all now.

Being in the army and propping up efforts to restore the infrastructure and keep people alive was at least something for which the likes of Gordon and Hawkins had trained. There had been some initial prickliness when they ended up in the same squad. Not because Aaliyah was black or female, but because Dave was part of the new intake when the Army and RAF merged. Traditional inter-departmental rivalry had to be overcome. He absorbed the piss-taking with as much grace as he could, and he learned to fit in. They were all after some kind of purpose, and the Army was always going to give them something to do now and again, even if there was a load of busy work as well.

As far as Aaliyah was concerned, the patrols qualified as makework. Nowadays, especially in a poxy little village in Norfolk, there wasn’t much to keep them busy. They maintained the curfew, checked the passes of anyone out and about after dark, but there hardly seemed to be any point to it. Public disorder was no longer the issue it was in recent years; people kept themselves busy during the day with farming and forestry and all the little ancillary trades that had come back, and by and large they were too knackered to do anything at night. A pint or two in the Dog and Partridge and then home. But they patrolled anyway, because those were their orders. And Sergeant Harry Bent was a stickler and proud of it. The patrols weren’t so bad anyway: there were far worse places to walk than a quiet village at night. The air had cleaned up quite a bit, apart from the decidedly rural stinks that rose up at various times of the year, and even that was bearable, almost wholesome. They knew plenty of the locals now, and, tempering their authority with politeness, weren’t given a hard time at all.

By the time Gordon and Hawkins were halfway done with this patrol, the second one of the evening, the night air had turned chilly. Not particularly bitter, but they would both be glad to get back indoors. The coal-fired boiler looked like a museum piece, something their grandfathers or great-grandads might have had to tend, but it kept the place warm. A cup of tea wouldn’t go amiss either; they had started to get the odd shipment in from India again, and you’d best believe the NAAFI made sure to get their share. Chalky was suitably religious about keeping the urn ready.

Both of them would have preferred coffee, but that wasn’t happening any time soon.

Their course took them down Wilby Road, past what had until recently been the West Farm Bed & Breakfast, catering to tourists, before converting back to West Farm, currently growing beans and peas.

“Huh. Full moon,” said Dave, looking up.

“Ooh, werewolves. Spooky.”

“Huh?”

“Werewolves. You know? Even a man who is pure of heart and says his prayers by night, may become a wolf when the wolfsbane blooms, and the moon is full and bright. Private Gordon missed her movies. She hoped someone could get the classics back onto film somehow, make some old style projectors.

“…I don’t know about that, Lee,” said Dave. “I don’t know about that at all. I don’t think I know nuffin’”.

Aaliyah followed his gaze. She looked up and let out a string of expletives that shocked even her.

The Moon was a grey disc, more charcoal than chalk, with jagged lines arcing over its surface.

Sandra Mason didn’t care for the curfew much. It’s not as if much went on in Todham, even now, but having to stay at home by order was an intrusion into her life and she resented it. Admittedly the soldiers enforcing it weren’t too bad. She was pretty friendly with them, almost on first-name terms, and tat night hey usually sent her on her way with a ‘you’d best get off home,’ but that was hardly the point. She had lived here all her life, made it her business to know her rights, and she noticed any curtailment of them.

She knew why the rule was in place, of course. There was that scare a couple of months ago; the Beast, they called it. A wolf, of all things, going after people’s livestock! She knew someone, well, sort of knew, a friend of a friend really, who’d lost a sheep to that. There had been a photo in the paper of the poor creature, torn to bits, partially eaten. Certainly not the work of poachers – and there’d been a few of them since the Fall. Probably the same bastards who stole farm equipment before then. Farmers were easy targets, miles from anywhere. All too easy to get in, grab whatever they could, and be long gone by the time the police were here. Assuming they had the fuel, of course; that was rationed now.

Sandra still made a point of being out and about at night. She found the patrols easy to anticipate, and even for a big girl in her forties like her, she could dodge them. It was something to do; a little act of defiance. People pretty much expected it from her.

In a perverse kind of way she rather hoped she’d catch someone trying to thieve her chickens. Just for the sake of putting a bit of stick about; just to show the last of the criminal element she wasn’t scared of them. She still had her shotgun, and while she was pretty sure she wouldn’t shoot someone over her chickens, she knew it’d be a good way to persuade them not to put up a fight or run away. She didn’t bring it with her on her walks around the village, but tonight she wished she had. Things seemed a bit… off. She wasn’t sure what, but she trusted her instincts and reckoned her eyesight would catch up with it soon enough. She’d feel better about it with her gun in her hands.

By the time Sandra was halfway down Barries Close, where the lane from the village proper branched off into Sage Cross, the half-finished new-build housing estate, she felt acutely aware of things being amiss. She blinked a few times, cursing herself for not keeping her eyes open. What was it, now? A sound out of place? The faint barking of dogs, James Stone’s two golden retrievers Joey and Pickles, but not much else beyond that. The murmur of a radio programme. Not so many stations nowadays but thank God there were still some. No, everything sounded normal.

It was something obvious, she just knew it. Because it’s always the obvious stuff you miss. ‘Can’t see it for looking at it,’ like Rob at the pub always said.

“Look up.”

She was not alone. A teenage girl had joined her; she thought it was Richard Benson’s kid. What was her name, now? Something daft, out of a fantasy film. Freya, that was it. What a millstone that must be! Kelly felt sorry for her already. Freya Benson. God, her schoolmates must have called her ‘Fray Bentos’ or worse. Sandra was just glad she called her own kid Charlotte. Much less bother.

“What?”

“Look up, Cassandra,” said Freya.

Well, didn’t that just come out of nowhere! “Who told you my name was Cassandra?” asked Sandra. She’d been carefully leaving the first syllable off her name for twenty years.

Freya laughed. Not maliciously, but it still unsettled her. She indicated a little further down the lane. An old woman, well, not exactly old, not these days, sixty-something was hardly old. Old enough for her leg to play up, though, and Sandra would recognise that gait anywhere. Moon Thomas. Her parents were hippies. She and Sandra had had each other’s sympathies for long enough to be friends.

“Bloody Hell. What is this, the Embarrassing Names Club?”

“I think the girl told you to look up, Sandy,” said Moon.

Sandra looked up, finally, and saw Moon’s namesake.

“Well, bugger me,” she said.

Moon and Frey looked up too. None of them felt the urge to panic; in fact the Moon didn’t seem to have anything wrong with it at all. In fact, comparing the Moon with how it used to look, it seemed more inviting than before. It drew the eye. It drew the whole body, really. Without a trace of self-consciousness, the three joined hands. A silvery aura suffused them, the faintest light shining forth from their every orifice and pore. It gave them a spectral aspect, replaced substance with radiance as it intensified.

Smiles of ecstasy spread across their faces.

And that was the last anyone saw of Cassandra Mason, Frey Benson, or Moon Thomas.

Mild queasiness simmered up to compete with White’s sense of dread as he obeyed the instructions given him. Not for the first time, he was struck by how the oddity of the new way of things had become the new normal. Just copy it down into a book, issue a load of files, stamp it a few times, and hey presto, demonology became as mundane as an equipment requisition form. Part of him wanted to stop, grab the world by its collective lapels, and protest about how insane the whole affair had become. A larger part of him conceded that point, but invited him to come forward with any better ideas he might have. A still larger part of him resolved to just get on with his job. Who knows what sort of trouble he’d get into if he didn’t?

Opening the supply cupboard on the first floor, and the instruction manual, White prepared to make the strangest phone call he would ever make.

STANDARD OPERATING PROCEDURE FOR CONTACTING THE ARS GOETIA CONTRACTORS, the first page read. This reassured Chalky. It was nicely bland and military; far less likely to evoke snorts of derision or disbelief than How to Summon Demons from Hell.

ESTABLISHING CONTACT WITH THE CONTRACTORS REQUIRES ADHERENCE TO PROTOCOL. THIS IS TO ENSURE YOU ARE IN THE CORRECT FRAME OF MIND TO PROCEED. IF YOU ARE RELIGIOUS YOU WILL UNDERSTAND THE IMPORTANCE OF RITUAL. IF NOT, TREAT THE MATTER AS SERIOUSLY AS A PUBLIC DISPLAY OF PRECISION DRILLING BEFORE FOREIGN DIGNITARIES.
BEGIN BY PREPARING YOURSELF AND THE AREA. TAKE A SHOWER AND FOLLOW THE INSTRUCTIONS PROVIDED.

Chalky winced, before remembering the boiler was lit. At least there would be hot water. Probably. He stripped off, and walked in. Affixed to the inside of the cubicle was a laminated sheet of paper, on which the following was printed:

WHILE SHOWERING, READ THE FOLLOWING:
ASPERGES ME, DOMINE, HYSSOPO ET MUNDABOR:
LAVABIS ME, ET SUPER NIVEM DEALBABOR

(Sprinkle me with hyssop, Lord, and I shall be cleansed;
Wash me, and I shall be whiter than snow)
THIS IS A RITUAL CLEANSING. TAKE A SET OF ROBES FROM THE SUPPLY CLOSET WHEN FINISHED.

He groaned. Was absolutely everything going to be a ritual from here on out? Well, he had sung sillier things in the shower, and now was no time to get all self-conscious. He sluiced down, chanting like a Catholic Priest all the while. Towelling off and redressing, White kept the book handy. He detected a distinctly religious undercurrent to all this magic: procedure required him not only to leave his boots off like a Muslim or Hindu, but to go entirely barefoot.

Sets of hooded robes laid folded on one of the shelves in the supply cupboard, made of heavy-duty white cotton. Naturally, the book had something to say about correct procedure of putting on the robes.

In the mystery of these vestures of the Holy Ones, I gird up my power in the girdles of righteousness and truth in the power of the Most High…

The list of paraphernalia called for by the book included a mitre, and a good deal of other things not in the cupboard. Presumably command decided that most of that was unnecessary. If his head had to be covered (yarmulkes, turbans, mitres, birettas, there was no end of religious headgear, although he was always told to take his hat off in church), presumably the hood would do.

WEAR THE SILVER RING AT ALL TIMES. IF AT ANY POINT YOU FEEL THREATENED, HOLD THE RING UP.

White read on, and headed for the supply cupboard to draw the various materials called for. He took some sticks of incense, five wax candles and plain brass candlesticks, a box of matches. He found the silver ring in a presentation box. A signet ring, stamped with the symbol of a six-pointed star within a circle, with a raised dot between each point. According to the book, this was the Seal of Solomon; while the manual attributed incalculable value to it, White would have thought fifty quid a more suitable offer; sixty if he was feeling generous.

The banality of the language, the reduction of the ritual to military procedure, had had its intended effect, and quietened his initial fear and panic. He hardly thought about the Moon at all. After all, what could he do about it personally? He could hardly shoot it with his SA-80. He just wished he could do something about his nagging feeling of absurdity. Perhaps he might feel more at home with the idea once he had taken that shower, and got his robes on, his boots off, and some joss sticks burning.

FIRST, NOTE THE CIRCLE OF SOLOMON. YOU WILL SEE A TRIANGLE ON THE EAST SIDE. INSIDE THIS TRIANGLE IS ANOTHER CIRCLE. THIS IS THE TRIANGLE OF ART. THE CONTRACTOR’S SEAL IS TO BE DRAWN IN CHALK INSIDE THAT CIRCLE. YOU WILL FIND THE SIGIL IN THE RELEVANT SECTION OF THE FOLDER MARKED ‘GOETIA.’

Chalky reached into the clear plastic wallet and leafed through the photocopied pages within. Sure enough, there was a seal: a circle, containing a figure made up of loops, spirals, and lines that intersected and bent at right angles. He grimaced. It looked like a bugger to draw. Still, taking up a stick of chalk, a ruler, and a set square, he got to work. Perhaps if he started with the straight bits, the rest wouldn’t look too difficult.

“White, this is Salmon. Report status, over.”

“Ritually cleansed and robed, Sir. Area consecrated with incense. Have drawn the, uh, Seal of -” he paused to remind himself, “Andrealphus in the Triangle of Art. Following instructions as written, Sir, candles ready, over.” He tried not to think about what he was doing as he reported it, but failed to keep the incredulous tone completely out of his voice.

“Good work. You see where to put the candles, over?”

“In the five-pointed stars. Clearly marked, Sir, over.”

“Light the candles when ready. The next section in the book should be the Bornless Ritual, over.”

“Copy that, Sir. Bornless Ritual.”

“Read the instructions through before trying anything, White. It’s going to feel bloody silly at first when you do this, but you mustn’t let that stop you. Where you ever in the school play?”

White noticed the lapse in radio discipline. “Couple of times, Sir, over.”

“It’s sort of like that,” said Salmon. “Speak up, project, and just concentrate on the script. You’ll have that right in front of you. The script as written in the left column, what it means in the middle column, and how it should sound on the right. Just like a phrasebook. Got it?”

“Yes, Sir.” White skimmed through the instructions, frowning. “Any other directions, over?”

“This ritual’s an appeal to what they call the Bornless One. Something to which all other spirits are subservient.”

“God, basically, Sir?”

“Pretty much. Once you appeal to him, then there’s the actual appeal to Andrealphus. It means when you call on Andrealphus, you’ll have the Bornless One’s authority and protection. If you like, think of it as letting him know Sergeant Bent is ready to give him a roasting if he tries anything funny.”

“Understood, Sir. Ready to proceed with Bornless Ritual, over.”

“Best of luck, White. Out.”

Privates Gordon and Hawkins had both taken turns at station duty and stood their share of skywatches, so they knew the drill if they saw something unnatural in the heavens. Panting, they barged through the door of the watch station, to find the lights on, but the desk unmanned.

“Where’s White?”

Hawkins shrugged and scanned the room. He opened doors, poked his head through, and Gordon soon joined him in the task.

“Oi! Ken!”

“Chalky?”

Here and there they saw evidence of activity. The radio had been used. One of the walkie-talkies was missing. The filing cabinet was unlocked, its top drawer open.

“Must be upstairs,” said Hawkins.

Aaliyah Gordon held out a hand palm downward, craned her head. “Shh,” she said. “Listen.”

They listened. There was a voice, White’s, reciting something. Though the sound came from upstairs, and was muffled by the ceiling, there was a kind of a rhythm to it. Not poetic as such, but long sentences with definite pauses in between, as if he was reading aloud.

Hawkins mouthed the words ‘what the fuck?’

Private Gordon, similarly silent, spread her hands and shrugged. She searched the desk, hoping that White had left a note. Chalky was usually good about doing things by the book.

“Is he buggering about with the Circle?” said Hawkins. “Bent’ll have his bollocks for that.”

Gordon found what she was looking for.

“Called Lt. Salmon about the Moon,” she read. “Have been ordered to use the Circle. Lt. Salmon is supervising by radio. Do not disturb or interrupt. Please man the desk until I relieve you.

“Chalky.

“P.S. Tea urn boiled at 2130. Biscuits in the tin.”

“What do you think, Lee?” asked Hawkins.

“It’s Chalky’s problem. Time for a brew-up.”

The Lieutenant set down his two-way radio, and accepted a mug of tea from Private Clarke. Nodding his thanks, he took a deep swig and cast an eye over the rest of the section. Everyone had shifted into high gear. Harrison, the platoon’s radio operator, looked frazzled but in her element, as she handed over another caller to Sergeant Bent. Another patrol had just come back in, faces pale, and he scarcely needed telling what they had to report. It was only a matter of time before the news spread all over Wenscombe; all any one had to do was look up, and then it was anyone’s guess what they would do. At least in the town there was a civil authority with whom he could liaise. Even if telephones were unreliable at best. Smartphones stopped working ages ago, and while the phone lines were still in place, the business of downgrading to a system less sophisticated but more robust was, to put it mildly, still ongoing. Lieutenant Dan Salmon could at least count as the blessing the fact that the military, the police, and those others involved in civil infrastucture could be deemed ‘high priority’ and thus had a landline.

He’d got back into the habit of calling it a telephone, rather than just a phone, since it was a permanent fixture to which access was restricted. It had substance. Purpose. Not just a glass-topped rectangle containing a load of apps that he never used. It still looked peculiar to him, even after sitting on the wall for a couple of years. The affair was retrofitted into the casing of a push-button phone, much like his parents used to have. This in turn was mounted on a box that contained a dynamo; powered by the caller turning the crank. The buttons did nothing. For the time being, no-one got to dial direct.

He picked up the receiver and wound the crank a few times, signalling the exchange.

“Operator. What number, please?”

“Wenscombe Garrison calling. Priority connect to Superintendent Keene at the police station, please.”

In the background, Lt. Salmon heard the chatter of other switchboard operators. All hands were on deck tonight. Three guesses why, first two don’t count…

“Connecting you now, Lieutenant.”

The whizz of a magneto being cranked. A click as he was patched through. The faint buzz of the line waiting. Another click.

“Wenscombe Police Station,” said a man’s voice on the other end, compressed and crackling. “Sergeant Morris speaking.”

“This is Lieutenant Salmon at the garrison. Is Superintendent Keene available?”

“One moment, Sir. I’ll transfer you now.”

Click. Buzz. A familiar female voice, educated, but still with a Norfolk twang. “Keene here.”

“The world’s changed a lot over the past few years. And what we’ve seen tonight is just proof that change is an ongoing process. A lot of things that we’ve taken for granted are gone, and as always we’ll have to adapt. Thank you very much.”

Luna frowned as she cast her eyes over the landscape that had been named after her, or had she been named after it? It didn’t matter. When you were a god there was no need to go around making sense all the time.

At first glance all was desolate, bleak; not a trace of the Moon’s old glory. It was as if nobody even remembered how things used to be. Well, they would in time. She did, after all. She looked down, and saw footprints in the pallid grey dust. Old ones. Had another god followed her after she went away? No, this was mortal work. They always left messes wherever they went. Whoever left it wore boots, big boots; with treads. Other tracks weaved this way and that, as if someone had driven a wagon all over the place.

Well, that simply wouldn’t do at all.

Raising a hand, palm upwards, she bade the dust to cover over every trace of this intrusion. It was surprisingly easy to undo. In this new age people were even prepared to disbelieve their own greatest achievements. She felt a twinge of regret once the footprints had vanished. After all, it was one of their accomplishments, wasn’t it, to walk on the moon? Just erasing it seemed disrespectful somehow. No, never mind that. When a mortal overstepped, you struck them down for their hubris. That was the way of things. But with so few people who visited the moon remaining, and all of them old, it was hardly worth asking Jupiter or Apollo to strike them down. They were no doubt seeing to their own abodes across the cosmos. Erasing evidence of the deed while they were still alive to remember. That would be harsh enough. That would be just.

Σελήνη rose up from beneath the dust to join her. She and Luna were close, close enough to be mistaken for each other. Ἑκάτη, Diana, Ἄρτεμις, too; there wasn’t really a word to describe what they were to each other. Offshoots of the same being, differentiated from each other, renamed as cultures diverged, cognates of each other, merged syncretically as their cults developed; they had no truck with such clinical terms, and long ago dispensed with the idea of a history that could never do them justice. You can’t be a goddess and go around making sense all the time. For conveniences’ sake they considered each other sisters. They began their dance, reshaping the Moon to their requirements, setting into motion seas that had never moved nor known a drop of water. They leapt in manners that defied gravity; especially easy since its hold was only a fraction of that on the Earth. They flew, they soared, they plummeted to the ground at terminal velocity but landed with balletic grace and no more impact than a feather.

They came together; they formed overlapping triads, slender limbs describing complex geometric, or rather selenometric figures. They merged into one; they separated into a multitude, their bodies and faces shifting like lunar phases.

The palaces and temples sprouted from the ground as if elevated from a trapdoor beneath the stage: gaudy, sparkling, spectacular. Colonnades and cloisters surrounded what, in the fullness of time, would be the most magnificent gardens, once things started growing. Not yet, though; the lunar soil was too parched and pale to nourish any kind of life. Still, the architecture was fitting: soon enough it would be suitable for her, her retainers and nymphs and sacred beasts, and the gods and goddesses that would visit. It could do with looking smoother, and the material was not the best: just feldspar, hardly what she’d call regal. She had to make do with what was available, and the Moon was far more desolate than she remembered. Sifting lunar dust through her pale fingers and letting it drift back down to the ground, she saw there were some suitable materials. She might conjure up moonstones from the feldspar, which would make for appropriate decoration. The occasional bead of volcanic glass hinted at the possibilities of windows, or better yet, towers of glass. But no silver. That was a shame; she liked silver.

There would be silver. She willed it.

Dust clouds hung in space. Then they began to hang in the air, as a sky began to render itself into being. Here and there, silver began to glimmer in the soil. The ground glowed with a soft, pearlescent light. Moonlight; not just the reflected rays of the Sun, borrowed like a cup of sugar from a neighbour, but true moonlight; home-made moonlight.

It was starting to look a lot more like home.
