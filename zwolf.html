Zwölf

In English, eleven and twelve mean ‘one left over’ and ‘two left over’, respectively. It’s the linguistic anxiety common to all Germanic languages. We like decimals, they make sense, they’re tidy, but we keep our dozens as well, just in case. Mostly.

French revolutionaries, not content with lopping the heads off the aristocrats, dismembered the clock’s hands and decimalised time.

Jacques-Marie Gaillard, despite his revolutionary courage, lived in fear of his equally formidable wife’s monthlies. He insisted that they abide by the French Republican Calendar. Madame Gaillard struck him dead with twelve blows of a cast-iron cocotte. He might have survived ten.

Decimal time lasted 17 non-decimal months before it ran out. Couldn’t even hold out for a nice round twenty.

Twelve endures; a number of tradition, completeness, order. Signs of the Zodiac, Apostles, Gods of Olympus, Labours of Heracles. Hours on a clock face. Anything less is an incomplete circle. Eleven and twelve aren’t left over. They’re reminders; leave them out at your peril.

Young Korasiobolos of Athens, seeking a skilled erastes to train him in arms, offered a bull to the Gods. Desirous of Athene’s favour, he stiffed Ares on his share of the sacrifice. Two nights later, the God of War sent him Tauromachus of Sparta, ‘the Orphanmaker’, who buggered his prospective eromenos halfway to Hades. Korasiobolos later fled to Corinth and opened a fish shop.

Time marches on: we decimalised our currency, we grudgingly went metric, and most printed multiplication tables only take us as far as 10×10 nowadays. Tidier? Yes. But is it safer?

In 1994 unemployed astrologer Gwyn Hughes decimalised the Zodiac, eliminating Aquarius and Pisces. Forty-eight hours later the police dragged his soggy carcass out of a waterbutt. Because of the cod lodged in his mouth and his fundament, locals thought he had died imitating the late MP Stephen Milligan.

Lately, supermarkets have started selling eggs in boxes of ten. Avian flu is on the rise. Naturally, the events are related; just not necessarily in the way we think.
