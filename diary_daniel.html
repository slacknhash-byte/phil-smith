The Diary of Daniel Turner

The original manuscript, significantly deteriorated and barely legible, was among a collection of personal effects belonging to my grandfather, Major Henry Austin of Thetford, late of the King’s Own Royal Regiment. He told my father that he found a diary in the Stanford Battle Area in June 1945, when he was a newly-minted Second Lieutenant. The diary was significantly faded when he discovered it, and is practically blank now; the record exists only because he made several copies throughout his life, each of which faded in turn, perhaps due to poor quality ink or paper. The copies my father made suffered similar fates, as have mine. For all the reader knows, this manuscript and my preamble could easily be an elaborate hoax. I claim otherwise but can offer no evidence to support that.

No record exists of a Daniel Turner in Thetford or Tottington during the war years. There was a James Robert Turner of Tottington, and his elder brother Stanley Henry Turner of the same, both attached to the 5th Battalion of the Royal Norfolk Regiment; the former killed and the latter captured, with James survived by his widow Maisie Turner (née Garner) who moved to Thetford when Tottington was cleared. She remarried in 1946, but died childless in 1982.

I leave it up to you to draw your own conclusions.

Monday 30th April, 1945

Funniest thing! I’d just got home from school, about to get stuck into my prep: geom. (yawn) and alg. (snore), when I heard an ARP Warden yell “TURN THAT FLAMING LIGHT OUT!” He didn’t sound like Mr. Green, who usually does our street. He wasn’t local, more cockney if anything. A right little jobsworth, stamping up and down like he owned the street. I looked out the window, couldn’t see anyone. But there it was again: “TURN THAT LIGHT OUT! DIDN’T YOU HEAR THE SIREN?!”

There wasn’t a siren. And it’s half-past four in the afternoon. Never mind blackout, it’s not even dark yet! It’s just someone mucking about. And I’m just making excuses not to get started with this algebra (Zzz…)

Besides, no more air raids for now, not with the Russkies in Berlin.

Tuesday 1st May, 1945

There’ll be no sleep for me tonight. I’m not sure if it’s excitement or just nerves. I’ve been like this since I found out Hitler bought it, and I bet everyone else has been too. Some admiral called ‘Dernitz’ or ‘Do’nuts’ or some such is their Fuehrer now. Make the most of it, you’ll have to give it up in a few days!

Alright, it’s not actually over, not properly over, and God knows when the Japanese will give up. And even if they do, is it over then? Maybe for Dad, he died in Singapore, but not for Uncle Stan and the others, who got captured.

I was talking about that outside school with the other 4th Form boys when old Bill Muteham tottered past on his way to the Anchor. “It’s never over,” he said. “This ‘un’s just the Great War carried on, and that ‘un carried over from all the others since Napoleon.” Joe Snelling says Old Bill’s touched in the head, and maybe he is, just a little, but he’s not wrong. I’m no sympathiser but everyone’s got a grudge to bear. The last one was meant to be the war to end all wars, wasn’t it?

I just heard it again! “TURN THAT FLAMING LIGHT OUT!” Stamp, stamp, stamp, stamp. It wasn’t funny the first time.

Eng. Lit. prep – not so bad really. Can’t say I like Dickens but reading a novel? That’s a doddle. It was the best of times, it was the worst of times? Alright, Charlie, you can have that one.

Wednesday 2nd May, 1945

Berlin surrendered, and the Germans in Italy have given up. Lambert the Loony Lit. Master hasn’t given up, though, he wants the rest of A Tale of Two Cities read and 500 words on Sidney Carton and his selflessness for next week. “It is a far, far better fate that I go to tomorrow if I don’t write this bloody essay…” And the usual torture of Latin prep. What the Hell is a gerund, anyway? I must have looked it up a thousand times since I started here and it still hasn’t stuck.

People have been hearing noises from over Tottington way. Joe Snelling says he heard a marching band, and an air-raid siren (I ask you!), but surely he’s pulling my leg. We’ve not had one for ages now. If there was an air raid siren, we’d hear it and be in the shelters until the all-clear.

“I heard what I heard,” says Joe. “There’s the tune, daah dum dum dah dum dum dah-dum dah-dum dah-dum, the band plays the last bit of the march – dum da dum dum, dah, dah, dah, dadadada dum dada dum – then the drums and cymbals– boom boom! Boom boom! Brr-rr-rr-rroom boom! Boom ba-doom boom boom-ba-dum-boom – and then the siren. Almost like it’s an instrument in the band.”

I didn’t recognise the tune. He didn’t either. Jolly catchy, though. “Maybe it’s a new tune,” he says. “For the parade, when that happens. Any day now, isn’t it?”

Winding me up or not, he does a pretty good impression of a band. Drum rolls and all. I could just about believe him. If the army have got a band over there, maybe things have settled down and Mum and I might even move back sometime. Thetford’s all right, there are much worse places to live, but like that girl says in that film, there’s no place like home. It was the last place I saw Dad before we waved him off.

Thursday 3rd May, 1945

Our boys took Hamburg. Can’t let the Russkies and the Yanks get all the glory!

Speaking of Ivan, this week’s Hist. prep: Discuss the effect of the Russian Revolution on the prosecution of the Great War. I think Barratt the Ancient wants the usual ‘commies aren’t reliable in a fight’ line. Not that they didn’t change their tune when the Russians joined in on our side…

On the subject of that marching band, I read this in today’s paper:

Captain Arthur Manning-Warren denied any suggestion of parade music being played in or around the Stanford Battle Area. Though victory in Europe is practically certain, no such music played near Stanford, Tottington, or West Tofts. Were any such rehearsal planned, it would likely have taken place at a site more convenient to the band members. When questioned further, he told the Thetford and Watton Times that any music heard could easily be explained away, probably as the noise of someone practising with a trumpet or playing a gramophone record, with the resultant sound carried on the wind. He urges the public to bear in mind that the Stanford Battle Area is still restricted and will remain so for the foreseeable future.

Friday 4th May, 1945

Monty’s accepted the surrender of the forces of Denmark, Holland, Northwest Germany, and it looks like the locals are jolly glad of it. Whether that’s ‘hurrah, we’re free’ or ‘hooray, the shooting’s stopped’, who knows? A little of both.

I might not know what a gerund is, but I still got top marks in that Latin test. God knows how. Barratt the Less Ancient didn’t mind my asking him afterwards, just this once. “It’s a word that’s formed as a verb but acts as a noun. And to ask again, Turner, is to risk a caning. A thrashing. A beating. There’ll be a wailing and gnashing of teeth, see if there isn’t!” I think those were examples of gerunds. He at least smiled when he said it.

The tune’s caught on. Am I alone in thinking this is all a bit odd? The song’s got lyrics now, and even some of the new bugs have learned it. As far as I can make out, it’s basically thumbing the nose at the Nazis, saying ‘yah-boo-sucks’ to Hitler. All well and good, but it’s a bit late in the day if you ask me. Now we’re asking him Who do you think you are kidding? I should think he’s past caring now!

It’s not a bad little song for all that. Mr Brown goes off to town on the 8:21, but he comes home each evening and he’s ready with his gun! The Home Guard could have done with this at the start. I’ve not heard the song in full, it’s not been on the wireless as far as I know, certainly not on Force’s Favourites. A few chaps think it might be a Flanagan and Allen song that’s just made a comeback, or a B-side, but nobody really agrees on anything.

Saturday 5th May, 1945

Well, that about wraps it up for the Nazis. Just the official surrender and trials and punishments to dole out, I guess. We’ve all heard of what the camps were like, and they’ve printed some pretty shocking stuff in the papers.

Just the same, this jolly little tune comes out of nowhere, and somehow everyone knows the words and the tune, even without it ever being played on the wireless or having the sheet music! I checked. And I’m not the only one to do so. People have been in and out of Parker’s asking for ‘that new Flanagan and Allen piece’ or Who Do You Think You Are Kidding, Mr Hitler? and leaving empty-handed or being fobbed off with a copy of Run Rabbit Run.

A song that nobody wrote but everyone’s whistling it. If I believed in ghosts, I’d say we were being haunted. Diary, I am rattled. I don’t want to make a fuss, and I’m certainly not telling the rest of the boys or I’ll get ragged all week. Shan’t let them think Dan’s barmy!

Sunday 6th May, 1945.

I thought I’d straightened myself out, stopped worrying, but I guess I’ve not had enough of being haunted yet.

A couple of Home Guard bods come out of the Anchor. Didn’t recognise either of them; a Captain and a Sergeant who must have been from out of town. Maybe that’s the elusive Captain Manning-Warren. Pudgy, pompous, fussy little moustache, round glasses, looked more like a bank manager than a soldier. His sergeant didn’t look like a sergeant, or what I’d expect from one – very nonchalant, easy-going, old public schoolboy. Mon Capitaine was outlining a plan to deter Nazi paratroops with fields full of scarecrows with very long pitchforks, and the sergeant questioned the wisdom of the idea. The officer cracked some sort of private joke: ‘oh, I don’t know, they don’t like it up ‘em,’ which got a polite chuckle. Then they rounded the corner to head down Riverside. I followed after them, just to see, but they were gone. They might as well never have been there.

I’d talk to someone in authority, but who’d believe me? Here’s some oik from the Grammar School, talking about how people have plucked a song out of the ether and treat it like an old hit even though it never existed? How some Home Guards nobody saw vanish after giving a little comic dialogue? They’d either say ‘we’ve got more important things to worry about,’ or, more likely, ‘you stupid boy.’ I’ll keep my suspicions to myself for the time being, thank you very much!

Monday 7th May, 1945

They’ve surrendered! And on top of that, we got the day off and no prep (joy of joys). It doesn’t mean it’s all over, of course, our boys are still fighting or else taken prisoner. But the greater part of the war is over.

The bells have been ringing all day, King Street and the Marketplace are packed. The tables aren’t exactly groaning but people are making a good show of it, even clubbing their rations together so they can make some decent cakes. Flags may be a bit faded and bunting a bit sparse, but it’s colour.

Here’s the thing, though. Our boys are still fighting so is it all a bit premature? Perhaps that’s not the point. It could very easily all be over soon. And celebrating is sort of an act of defiance, a way to put two fingers up to the war, to show them we’re not beaten yet.

Some people brought their gramophones out. Nobody’s got a record of the song, but it’s not stopped some from trying to busk it on the piano or the accordion.

I --

I heard the band play it when it started to get dark. The wind must have changed, but there it was, coming over from Tottington. And I wasn’t the only one who heard it, either. Some folks sang along. Then there was the siren at the end. Very faint, but it was there. Joe Snelling, I owe you an apology, old man.

I’ve got to find out what’s at the bottom of it. I know we’re not supposed to, but since everyone’s in a party mood I’m tempted to sneak out to the old village tonight.

Don’t think they’d hang me just because I’m looking. Not a fifteen-year-old boy.
I’m just a boy up to hijinks, playing games.
Clip me ‘round the ear and then send me home again!
Can’t get too cross with me while they’re celebrating. Even then it’s just a band
